History

Skating

The origin of the skateboard is as ambiguous as the origin of our Universe. Yet most agree it began in the United States as simple wooden crates or planks fitted with roller skate wheels, evolving from early scooter-like designs into surf-inspired boards popular on Southern California hills in the 1950s. Before commercial boards existed, skating was entirely DIY, driven by personal creativity and self-expression—foundations that continue to define skateboarding today.

Famous Skaters

Six of the Most Famous and Influencial Skaters Include:

Tony Hawk
Revolutionized vertical skateboarding, first to land the 900 in competition, and became a global face of the sport through media and video games.
Rodney Mullen
Pioneer of modern street and freestyle tricks; invented foundational moves like the kickflip, 360 flip, heelflip, and “impossible,” shaping how skating is done today.
Mark Gonzales
Early professional who defined modern street skating by bringing creativity to urban terrain and popularizing city street expression.
Nyjah Huston
Dominant champion in professional street contests, known for technical precision, unmatched consistency, and a long-term competitive reign.
Eric Koston
Highly respected street professional whose effortless style and technical ability influenced skateboarding’s modern era including digital content and progression.
Bob Burnquist
Vert pioneer and key figure in massive aerial terrain like mega ramps, known for fearless height, creativity, and long-lasting impact.

Surfing

A long time ago in ancient Polynesia: latter-day Tahiti, perhaps; maybe Samoa or Tonga – or perhaps much further north by the dusky shores of ancient Hawaii – locals gazed at the raging whitewash lapping upon their island sands. Swimming, until then, had been the main pastime, but now they paddled into the ocean with planks of their finest wood, and with zest and zeal, began to take turns riding fiercely down the barrels of untamed swells and building sets. Over the centuries, Hawaiians refined this “wave sliding” into a sacred cultural practice, blending skill, ritual, and community. As the modern era emerged, surfing began to capture the imagination of outsiders. By the late 19th century, tourists on Waikiki Beach marveled at locals carving down waves, while Hawaiian princes demonstrated the sport in California, leaving crowds amazed. Writers like Mark Twain and Jack London even tried their hand—unsuccessfully—helping to introduce surfing to the Western world.

Famous Surfers

Six of the Most Famous and Influencial Surfers Include:

Kelly Slater
Dominant competitive surfer, 11× world champion, known for longevity, innovation, and mainstream global recognition.
Laird Hamilton
Big-wave pioneer who pushed surfing into extreme territory, helping develop tow-in and hydrofoil surfing.
Duke Kahanamoku
Early international ambassador of modern surfing, one of the first global icons to popularize the sport worldwide.
John John Florence
2× world champion famous for elite contest results and effortless style in heavy Hawaiian surf.
Tom Curren
3× world champion and style icon known for smooth rail work and influence on 80s–90s surf culture and music.
Bethany Hamilton
Globally recognized for her inspiring return to elite-level surfing after surviving a shark attack.
